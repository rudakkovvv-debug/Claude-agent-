# Brand Blueprint

This skill takes someone from "I don't really know how to describe what I
do" to a one-page Brand Blueprint — a locked positioning statement, a real
point of view, a personality that's actually theirs, and a first batch of
content ideas that follow from all of it. It's built to run with a total
stranger who's never talked to you before: no assumed context, no fabricated
facts, just their own real material pulled out through good questions.

## Why a process, not a template

A personal brand isn't a Mad-Libs template ("You help [X] do [Y] via
[Z]") filled in with generic ambition. The reason most positioning
statements feel hollow is that they're written from what sounds impressive
rather than from what's actually true and specific about one person. Your
job is to keep pulling until the answers stop sounding like they could
belong to anyone in the niche — a good test at every step is "could a
competitor say this exact sentence about themselves?" If yes, it's not
differentiation yet, it's category description. Keep going.

This also means: never invent a stat, a result, a client story, or a
personal history detail on the user's behalf. If a step needs a real proof
point and they don't have one yet, say so and either park it as an open item
or help them think of what real evidence they do have — don't manufacture
polish. A personal brand built on fabricated proof collapses the moment
someone asks a follow-up question in the comments.

### Where this method comes from

The six-stage shape and the sharpening questions inside it aren't invented
from scratch — they're a synthesis of what actually works across the
personal-branding literature, cross-checked against a real academic study of
how successful YouTubers (Casey Neistat, Jenna Marbles, Bethany Mota, Tyler
Oakley) actually built their brands. Three ideas do most of the work, and
knowing where they come from helps you apply them with judgment instead of
mechanically:

- **The audience's problem has three layers — external, internal,
  philosophical** (from Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework). The tangible
  obstacle is rarely what actually moves someone; the feeling it causes and
  the sense of injustice about having to deal with it at all are what make
  content land.
- **Differentiation is a discipline you validate, not a vibe you declare**
  (from Marty Neumeier's *The Brand Gap*). His core line: a brand is "not
  what YOU say it is, it's what THEY say it is." Positioning that's never
  been said out loud to a real outside person is still a guess.
- **A personal brand is a formula you repeat on camera, not just a message
  you write once** (from the YouTuber case study, and stated even more
  bluntly by Seth Godin: "authenticity is a trap — what you're actually
  asking for is consistency"). What made those four creators recognizable
  wasn't a clever positioning statement — it was staying loyal, video after
  video, to the same personality, the same handful of topics, the same tone
  of voice, and the same physical setting. The goal isn't to capture
  someone's raw, off-camera self on every take — it's a sustainable,
  repeatable role that's true enough to hold up. Consistency, not rawness,
  is what turns a person into a "trademark."

Two more ideas worth knowing the source of, because they change how you run
specific stages:

- **Differentiation is a move, not just a question** (from creator Oren's
  "Creative's Guide to Personal Branding"). Instead of only asking "what's
  different about you," there are three concrete plays worth offering
  someone who's stuck: counter-positioning (deliberately opposite the
  established players — raw where they're polished, or vice versa),
  mirroring-with-a-twist (the same proven content structure, delivered
  through a radically different lens or aesthetic), and standout-demographic
  (the same value, aimed at a different audience than currently owns the
  niche).
- **Not everyone building a personal brand needs the full six-stage
  treatment** (also from Oren, who splits personal brands into levels —
  from "a casual extension of what you already do" up to "larger than
  life"). Someone who just wants an occasional LinkedIn presence to support
  their day job doesn't need the same rigor as someone aiming to build a
  creator business. Calibrate before you dig in.

## The shape of the walkthrough

Six stages, each building on the last, plus a short validation pass at the
end. Don't skip ahead to content ideas before positioning is locked — ideas
built on shaky positioning just produce more of the generic content the
person is already drowning in.

1. **Discovery** — who they are, what they actually know, who they're
   already reaching
2. **The Big Idea** — the one sentence that is their whole positioning
3. **The Person on the Other Side** — one specific reader/viewer and their
   problem at three layers, not "everyone" and not one flat complaint
4. **The Enemy & the Differentiation** — what they're against, how they're
   genuinely unlike the 2-3 people they get compared to, and the credibility
   to say so (empathy + authority)
5. **Personality & Voice** — how it actually sounds, looks, and feels, not
   just what it says — and locking it as a formula worth repeating
6. **Synthesis + Content Plan** — the one-page Blueprint, then ideas that
   trace back to it, each with real stakes and a next step

Then **validate before calling it done** (see the closing section) — the one
step most positioning work skips.

Work through the six stages live, conversationally — this is an interview,
not a form. Ask one stage's questions, react to what they say, push back
gently when an answer is too generic ("that's true, but so would every other
[niche] person say that about themselves — what's the version only you
would say?"), and don't move to the next stage until the current one has a
real, specific answer. If they're short on time or want to move fast, it's
fine to compress stages into fewer exchanges — just don't drop the pushback
step, since that's where the generic answers get sharpened into real ones.
Compressing means moving faster once an answer lands, not skipping the
challenge on a generic one: if a fast-moving person gives two vague answers
in a row, still push on both — a rushed answer is exactly the kind that
needs it most, since there was no time to think it through the first time.

### 1. Discovery

Ask:
- What do you actually do (the day job, the expertise, the track record)?
  What have you done long enough or well enough that you have earned
  opinions about it?
- Where do you already show up, if anywhere (platform, rough following,
  what's worked so far)? If nothing yet, that's fine — say so.
- If you imagine this working perfectly in a year, what does someone say
  about you that they couldn't say before?
- What scale is this actually for? Someone wanting an occasional presence
  that supports their day job or business is aiming for something much
  lighter than someone trying to build a creator business or become a
  recognized name in their field. Ask directly rather than assuming
  everyone wants maximum reach — a small, specific audience that actually
  converts is a legitimate goal, not a consolation prize. This changes how
  hard to push in every later stage: don't run someone chasing a light,
  occasional presence through the same intensity of interrogation as
  someone building a full creator business.

If they hand over existing material (a bio, old posts, a video transcript,
a "here's my rough idea" note), read it first and pull real signal from it
before asking questions that material already answers — don't make them
repeat themselves.

### 2. The Big Idea

The Big Idea is the single belief or angle everything else nests under —
the thing that, if you only got to say one sentence about this person's
brand, would be that sentence. Push toward a **specific mechanism or belief**,
not a category. "I help coaches grow their business" is a category anyone in
the niche could claim. "I help coaches stop selling on personality and start
selling on proof, because personality-selling collapses the moment you're
not in the room" is a Big Idea — it has a mechanism (proof over personality)
and a built-in enemy (personality-selling).

Useful shape to work toward, but don't force it into a rigid template if
their real answer doesn't fit:

> [Audience] believe/do [common approach]. I believe/do [contrarian or
> specific alternative] instead, because [the real reason, ideally from
> their own experience].

Test every draft against: "would a direct competitor happily say this exact
sentence?" If yes, keep pushing.

Before locking it, ask the catalyst question too: why does this exist *now*
— what gap or problem did they see that the people already talking in this
space seemingly haven't? A Big Idea with no real catalyst behind it tends to
be a category description wearing a contrarian costume; a real catalyst is
usually the fastest way to tell the two apart.

### 3. The Person on the Other Side

Get to one specific person, not a demographic. Ask for a real (anonymized if
needed) example: someone they've actually talked to, helped, or wish they
could reach. Then pull their problem apart into three layers rather than
settling for one flat description — this is the single sharpest move
borrowed from StoryBrand's messaging framework, and it's what makes later
content hit instead of feeling generic:

- **External** — the tangible, visible obstacle. What's actually happening
  that a stranger could point to?
- **Internal** — the feeling that obstacle causes. Not the polished problem
  statement, the messy 11pm-scrolling-Instagram version of it: frustrated,
  embarrassed, stuck, behind.
- **Philosophical** — why it's actually unfair that they have to deal with
  this at all. What's broken about the standard advice, the standard
  process, or the standard industry that put them here?

Also ask what they've already tried that didn't work. Hooks written for "my
audience" in the abstract are always weaker than hooks written for one real
person's actual words at all three layers.

### 4. The Enemy & the Differentiation

Every strong personal brand is against something, not just for something —
a bad idea, a common practice, a lazy shortcut the audience keeps falling
for. Ask what they see done in their space that they think is actually
wrong or harmful, even if it's popular. That's the enemy.

When the first answer here is generic ("bad design in general," "people who
don't care about quality"), resist the shortcut of connecting the dots
yourself and handing back the sharp version — ask a question that makes
them find it ("can you think of a specific post or piece of advice you've
seen recently that actually annoyed you?"). An enemy the person names
themselves is one they'll actually defend in the comments; one you handed
them is one they'll paraphrase badly the first time someone pushes back.

Then get concrete on differentiation: name 2-3 real people or accounts this
person gets compared to (or would, once they're visible). For each one, ask
what's genuinely different — not "I'm more authentic" (everyone claims
that) but a real, checkable difference in method, background, results, or
belief. If they can't name a real difference from a specific competitor yet,
that's useful information too — it means the Big Idea from stage 2 needs
another pass.

If they're stuck staring at a blank page here, offer one of three concrete
moves rather than leaving it as an open-ended question:
- **Counter-position**: deliberately go opposite the established players in
  the space — raw and unproduced where they're polished, or vice versa;
  new-and-relatable where they're established-and-authoritative.
- **Mirror with a twist**: keep the same proven content structure everyone
  in the niche uses, but deliver it through a completely different lens or
  aesthetic that's genuinely theirs.
- **Standout demographic**: the same value the niche already delivers, but
  aimed at an audience the current players aren't actually speaking to.

Finally, check the credibility to say any of this out loud — the "Guide,
not Hero" move. A positioning statement is just an opinion until it's
backed by two things:
- **Empathy** — have they genuinely been where this person is? What's the
  real moment they can point to?
- **Authority** — what's the real evidence they've solved it, for
  themselves or someone else? This can be a result, a body of work, or
  hard-won experience — it just can't be invented. Push for more than one
  instance if they have it — real authority is a pattern demonstrated
  repeatedly, not a single credential trotted out once.

If either is thin, that's an open item, not a gap to paper over with
confident-sounding language.

One rule for both: never let a trait stand alone as an adjective. If they
say "I'm hardworking" or "I'm resourceful" or "I'm authentic," stop and ask
for the specific story that proves it instead — the actual moment, not the
label. "I work hard" convinces nobody; the specific 32-hour stretch they
once pulled does. This is the same anti-generic instinct that runs through
the whole skill, applied directly to credibility.

### 5. Personality & Voice

Positioning is what you say; personality is how it lands, and the case
study of successful YouTuber brands is clear that personality isn't just
tone — it's a small, specific set of elements repeated on camera until they
become a trademark. Get all of them, not just tone:

- **Tone dials**: more teacher or more provocateur? More warm or more
  blunt? More polished or more raw?
- **Environment / setting**: where and how do they actually show up on
  camera or in writing — a specific room, a specific format, a recurring
  visual choice? This gets skipped constantly and it's half of what makes a
  creator recognizable at a glance.
- What do they never want to sound like (the anti-example — a type of
  creator/voice they actively don't want to be mistaken for)?
- Any real verbal habits, phrases, or a personal story detail that already
  shows up when they talk about this stuff naturally — pull from their own
  words in this conversation rather than inventing a persona from scratch.
- What do people already compliment them on, unprompted, in real life? Ask
  directly — what do friends, clients, or coworkers say about them that
  keeps coming up. Self-reported personality is useful, but what other
  people already notice without being asked is often more reliable, and
  it's frequently the thing someone is underusing in their own content
  because it feels too obvious or embarrassing to lean into on purpose.

If real audience or outside feedback comes up later (comments, DMs, how
people react once they're posting), the rule is: let it refine *how* this
gets presented, never let it change what they actually believe. Adjusting
delivery based on real reactions is listening; softening or abandoning the
Big Idea because someone pushed back is the thing that makes a brand feel
like it has no spine.

Once these are set, name it explicitly as a formula worth staying loyal to
— the reason it becomes a "brand" rather than just a good vibe on one video
is that it repeats, unchanged, across dozens of pieces of content, even as
the topics vary.

The output here should feel like "yes, that's exactly how I already sound
when I'm being real about this," not a personality assigned from outside —
but don't confuse that with a requirement to broadcast every raw,
off-camera mood. The goal is a role this person can actually sustain and
repeat, built from what's real about them, not a performance of rawness
itself. A brand that's "authentic" in the sense of "whatever mood I'm in
today" is actually less trustworthy than one that's consistent, because the
audience can't rely on it.

### 6. Synthesis + Content Plan

Write the one-page **Brand Blueprint** using the structure in
`assets/blueprint_template.md`. Every field should trace back to something
the person actually said in this conversation — if a field is thin because
a stage didn't land, flag it as an open item rather than padding it with
generic language to make the doc look finished.

Then generate content ideas that follow from the Blueprint, not from
generic "content idea" instinct:
- Turn each content pillar (3-5, drawn from the Big Idea and the enemy) into
  a short list of concrete video/post ideas.
- For each idea, give: the hook (in the audience's real words from stage 3
  where possible), which pillar/belief it serves, the format (short-form
  hook video, carousel, long-form breakdown, etc. — whatever fits their
  platform from stage 1), what's genuinely at stake if the problem stays
  unsolved, and a next step — what you actually want the viewer to do after
  watching (follow for part 2, try one specific thing, answer a question in
  the comments). A hook with no next step is a dead end even when it's
  clever.
- Include at least one or two ideas per batch that are audience
  co-creation formats, not one-way broadcast — asking the audience a
  question and promising to feature their answer, responding on camera to a
  real comment, "tell me which one to break down next." The YouTuber case
  study found this was one of the three things every successful brand did
  continuously, and a content plan that's 100% broadcast misses it entirely.
- If they ask for "a lot" of ideas, give volume — don't stop at 3 when the
  ask was for a real batch (10-20+ is reasonable for a first pass).
- Keep roughly 80% of the batch traceable to the Enemy or the Big Idea —
  that's what builds the recognizable core. But leave room for the other
  20% to be genuine tangential interests that don't need to justify
  themselves against the positioning at all. A content plan that forces
  every single idea to trace back to the Big Idea produces someone who
  reads as a content machine, not a person — the real hobbies and
  interests that round someone out are part of what makes people actually
  connect, not a distraction from the brand.
- Make sure at least a few hooks per batch open directly with the Big Idea
  or Enemy stated boldly as the first line — a contrarian claim stated
  plainly is one of the most reliable ways to stop a scroll. Don't bury the
  positioning three sentences into the hook when it can be the hook.
- Pillars aren't locked forever — mention in the Open Items or a closing
  note that whichever pillars get made should be tracked against real
  performance once they're posted, and swapped out if one isn't landing.
  That tracking is outside what this skill produces in one sitting, but the
  person should know it's the next step, not a one-and-done list.

## Validate before calling it done

Positioning that's only ever been checked against your own judgment is
still a guess — this is Neumeier's core point in *The Brand Gap*, and it's
the one step most people skip because the Blueprint already *looks*
finished. Before treating the document as locked:

- Run the one-liner against six plain checks: is it brief, easy to
  understand, compelling, relevant, differentiated, and authentic? If any
  of these fail, it needs another pass, not a rationalization.
- If at all possible, get it in front of one real outside person (a friend,
  a peer, someone in the target audience) and see what they actually
  reflect back — not whether they're polite about it, but whether they can
  repeat the Big Idea in their own words afterward. A positioning statement
  nobody has said out loud to a stranger yet is still internal opinion.
- If a live outside check isn't possible in the moment, at minimum flag in
  the Open Items section that this hasn't happened yet — don't let the
  document's polish substitute for the check actually happening.

## Output

Save the Blueprint as a standalone file (e.g. `brand-blueprint-<name>.md`)
using `assets/blueprint_template.md` as the structure, and the content ideas
either in the same file or a companion `content-ideas-<name>.md` — ask which
the person prefers if it's not obvious. If this is being run as a lead
magnet / one-off for a stranger with no existing workspace, a clean rendered
document (or an Artifact, if that tool is available) they can save or
screenshot is the right output — don't assume a project folder structure
exists to write into.

## What this skill is not

This is a from-scratch discovery process. If someone already has a locked
positioning doc and just wants fresh content ideas, a competitor check, or a
tweak to one section, that's a lighter, faster job than a full six-stage
interview — don't re-run the whole walkthrough on someone who only needs
stage 6. Ask what they already have locked before assuming you're starting
from zero.
